# Cognitive Governance — Field Definition

**HumanTrace — Internal Document**
Version 1.0 · 30 March 2026 · Owlume Pty Ltd

---

## Definition

**Cognitive governance** is the discipline concerned with protecting the integrity of human judgment in environments where AI-generated reasoning is present at scale. It defines the standards, methods, and infrastructure by which institutions can:

1. Verify whether the reasoning behind a communication reflects genuine human cognition
2. Detect when that reasoning has been synthetically engineered to exploit human trust
3. Maintain accountability for decisions made under conditions of potential cognitive interference

---

## Why This Discipline Is Necessary Now

Existing governance frameworks address what AI *produces* — outputs, decisions, and recommendations made by automated systems. They do not address what AI-assisted reasoning does to the humans who receive it.

As AI generation becomes indistinguishable from human authorship, a new class of risk emerges: synthetic reasoning engineered to exploit the cognitive mechanisms by which humans trust, defer, and decide. No existing regulatory framework governs this risk. Cognitive governance names the discipline that does.

---

## Scope

| Domain | What it covers |
|---|---|
| Reasoning authenticity | Verifying that the reasoning behind a communication reflects genuine human cognition rather than AI-generated or AI-optimised content |
| Synthetic persuasion detection | Identifying when reasoning has been engineered to exploit trust, bypass critical evaluation, or induce action through cognitive interference rather than legitimate persuasion |
| Cognitive accountability | Maintaining defensible records of the cognitive conditions under which consequential decisions were made, for regulatory, legal, and operational purposes |

---

## How Cognitive Governance Differs from Adjacent Disciplines

| Discipline | Its primary concern | What it does not address |
|---|---|---|
| AI Safety | Preventing AI systems from causing harm through their own decisions or actions | The harm caused when humans are manipulated into harmful actions by AI-generated reasoning directed at them |
| Cybersecurity | Protecting systems, data, and infrastructure from unauthorised access or compromise | The cognitive compromise of human decision-makers who have not been technically breached but have been reasoning-breached |
| Content moderation | Identifying and removing harmful, false, or policy-violating content from platforms | The reasoning structure of a communication and whether its persuasive mechanisms are legitimate or exploitative |
| AI transparency / XAI | Making AI decision-making explainable and auditable | The authenticity of human-attributed reasoning in communications received by humans from other apparent humans |

---

## The Governance Gap

Financial services, legal practice, and regulated professional advice all operate on the presumption that the reasoning behind a communication reflects the genuine judgment of a responsible human.

ASIC's conduct obligations, APRA's prudential standards, and the Corporations Act's disclosure requirements each assume this. None were written for an environment in which that presumption can no longer be made without verification.

Cognitive governance is the infrastructure that closes this gap.
